Why Philippines?

The Philippines is a Southeast Asian archipelago country made up of over 7,000 islands scattered across the Pacific Ocean. The Philippines is blessed with lengthy stretches of immaculate white sand beaches bordered by crystal clear waters because to its long coastline. Not only that, but everyone is really really friendly!

World-renowned natural wonders such as an underground river and rice terraces, incredible diving spots rich in biodiversity, colorful public transportation, unique awesome cuisine, vibrant festivals that showcase the country's colorful culture, and friendly locals regarded as some of the happiest in the world can all be found in the Philippines.

So what are you waiting for?

Tourism History of PH

The Philippines' tourism industry dates back to ancient times, when the first group of people opted to travel across land bridges, followed by migrations from the Malayan archipelago in the south and Taiwan in the north. Numerous ethno-linguistic groupings arose over time, some of which eventually evolved into monarchs, plutocracies, hunter-gatherers, city-states, and so on.

Moreover, Arabs, Indians, Japanese, Chinese, Malays, and other ethnic groups in mainland Southeast Asia, Taiwan, and Ryukyu sold commodities with the natives, which formed a feature of tourism. When the islands became part of Spain's empire, an influx of Spaniards arrived, though it was relatively small compared to Spanish migrations in South America because the Philippines was further away.



Due to an inflow of immigration from Europe and the United States in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the tourism sector really took off. Apart from Hong Kong and Japan, it was named one of the best nations to visit in Asia, gaining the moniker "Pearl of the Orient Seas." During and during World War II, tourism decreased, leaving the country with a completely decimated economy and a landscape littered with abandoned heritage villages.



According to the secretary of Department of Tourism (DOT), Bernadette Romulo-Puyat,

The second wave of tourism peaked in the 1950s, but it plummeted during the dictatorship period. Due to the dictatorship's domino effect, the tourism industry continued to collapse after the People Power Revolution. Only in 1991 and 1992, when 1.2 million visitors visited the Philippines, did the business able to keep up. After a decade, it began to fade again as a result of government corruption.





The tourism sector grew for the third time in the early 2010s, thanks to the "It's More Fun in the Philippines" motto, which was generally hailed as a global success and garnered international media coverage. The country saw an influx of tourists from all over the world, and with the help of social media and a catchy tagline, tourism peaked in 2015, with 5,360,682 foreign million tourists recorded.
